# 1.1 The Nature and Uses of History

---

## A. Nature of History

The term **history** is derived from the Greek word *Istoria*, meaning *“inquiry”* or *“an account of one’s inquiries.”* The first use of the term is attributed to the ancient Greek historian **Herodotus (c. 484–425 B.C.E.)**, who is often regarded as the *“father of history.”*

In ordinary usage, history refers to all the things that have happened in the human past. The past signifies events that have taken place and the facts of the past that are preserved in writing.

More specifically, a distinction exists between:

- What actually happened in the past (events that occurred independently of historians), and  
- The accounts of the past written by historians — that is, *history*.

Historians apply their expertise to surviving records and write history as organized accounts of the past. Academically, history can be defined as:

> An organized and systematic study of the past.

The study of history involves:
- Discovery of evidence  
- Collection of information  
- Organization of data  
- Presentation of past events  

---

### The Scope of Historical Study

What actually happened in the past is almost infinite. Therefore, historians must select the topics and problems they wish to study, just as natural scientists do.

The major concern of history is the study of **human society and its interaction with the natural environment**. While many other disciplines also study this interaction, history differs because:

- Other disciplines focus mainly on the present.
- History studies human-environment interaction in the **past**, within the continuous process of change over time.

---

### Periodization in History

Because of the vast span of time, historians divide the human past into discrete periods. This is done after identifying significant developments in:

- Politics  
- Society  
- Economy  
- Culture  
- Environment  

These developments are studied through documents and artifacts left behind by earlier societies.

Each historical period is given a label that reflects its key characteristics. Conventionally, history is divided into:

- **Ancient History**
- **Medieval History**
- **Modern History**

This division is called **periodization**, one of the key characteristics of the discipline of history.

---

### Continuity and Change

When historians discuss continuity or persisting patterns, they do not mean that:

- A pattern applied to everyone everywhere, or  
- Nothing changed at all.  

All aspects of human life — social, cultural, economic, and political — have changed over time. However, some elements remain relatively stable for long periods because few things ever change completely.

For example, people often continue to:

- Speak the languages of their ancestors  
- Follow inherited religious beliefs and practices  
- Wear traditional costumes  
- Practice agricultural or pastoral lifestyles  
- Maintain fundamental social structures  

Similarly, the basic fabric of society in **Ethiopia and the Horn** remains similar and retains special characteristics despite historical change.

## B. Uses of History

People live in the present and plan for the future. History, however, studies the past. Why should we bother with the past while living in the present?

The value of history lies in the relationship between the past and the present.

---

### 1. History Helps Us Better Understand the Present

History serves as the primary storehouse of information about how people behaved in the past.

Understanding many modern problems requires tracing their origins in history. In other words:

> Knowledge of relevant historical background is essential for a balanced and in-depth understanding of current world situations.

---

### 2. History Provides a Sense of Identity

Knowledge of history helps us understand:

- Who we are  
- Where we fit in the world  

Just as memory is essential to an individual, history is essential to a society.

- An individual without memory struggles to relate to others and make wise decisions.  
- A society without history faces similar difficulties.

Through historical awareness, communities define their identity, orient themselves, and understand their relationship with both the past and other societies.

---

### 3. History Provides Background for Other Disciplines

Historical knowledge is valuable for the study of:

- Literature  
- Art  
- Philosophy  
- Religion  
- Sociology  
- Political Science  
- Anthropology  
- Economics  

History provides the foundational context necessary for these fields.

---

### 4. History Teaches Critical Skills

Studying history develops important academic and professional skills, including:

- Finding and evaluating sources  
- Analyzing different types of evidence  
- Constructing coherent arguments  
- Writing clearly and effectively  

These analytical and communication skills are transferable to many areas of life.

---

### 5. History Develops Tolerance and Open-Mindedness

Many people tend to view their own cultural practices as superior or correct. Studying other societies in the past is like visiting a foreign country.

It helps individuals:

- Overcome cultural narrowness  
- Develop broader perspectives  
- Gain flexibility in thinking  

Students of history acquire a wide range of perspectives useful in many life situations.

---

### 6. History Supplies an Endless Source of Fascination

Exploring how people in distant times constructed their lives offers:

- Intellectual excitement  
- Aesthetic appreciation  
- A deeper understanding of human society  

---

## Conclusion

History is essential for both individuals and society.

Only through studying history can we understand:

- How and why change occurs  
- What elements of society persist despite change  

While history can be useful, it can also be abused. Abuse occurs when the past is deliberately manipulated to fit present political agendas. In such cases, history is written backwards — the past is interpreted to justify the present.

Although personal biases may not always be avoidable, a true historian differs from a propagandist because:

> A historian documents evidence carefully so that claims can be independently verified.

This leads to an important question:

**How do historians study and interpret the past, especially periods in which they did not live?**
